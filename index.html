Agency Funding Program

Georgetown awards discretionary grants to outside non-profit and community organizations each fiscal year. Any recommendation by the Mayor is subject to availability of funds and requires City Council approval. This is separate from federal/state grants the city applies for directly.

1
Application Opens
City publishes Agency Funding Application, Budget Request Form, and Conflict of Interest Certification. Applications open annually — FY26 deadline was April 25, 2025 at 4:00pm.
2
Mayor Reviews
Mayor Jenkins reviews all applications. No funding is guaranteed. Recommendations are made based on availability of funds and alignment with city priorities.
3
Council Approves
Any agency funding recommended by the Mayor must be approved by City Council. Approved agencies execute a written agreement with the City before receiving funds.
4
Disbursement
Funds are disbursed under the written agreement for the fiscal year (July 1 – June 30). Agencies must report on use of funds per the agreement terms.

Georgetown Commons — Mixed-Use Development
Bringardner farm · 56,585 sq. ft. grocery + retail · Frankfort Pike & McClelland Circle
Preliminary Approved
The Georgetown-Scott County Planning Commission approved the preliminary subdivision plan for Georgetown Commons, a mixed-use development on the Bringardner farm at Frankfort Pike and McClelland Circle. The plan includes a 56,585 sq. ft. grocery store with an attached liquor store, plus a 7,000 sq. ft. retail tenant building on 12.1 acres. Four new access points are planned: two to Frankfort Pike, one from McClelland Circle, and one to Fincastle Drive. A traffic light would likely be required at the Elkhorn Crossing School intersection — final determination from the Kentucky Transportation Cabinet.

Triple Crown Regional Business Park
Multi-county park · Scott + Fayette counties voted yes · Richmond opted out
Regional Jobs TBD
The Triple Crown Regional Business Park is a multi-jurisdictional economic development project spanning Scott, Fayette, and Madison counties. Scott County and Fayette County voted to participate. Oct 2025
Housing Needs Assessment — Shortfall & Rising Demand
Consultants find persistent housing shortage · Senior & rental units needed · Land bank recommended
Policy
Georgetown and Scott County officials received a final housing needs assessment on October 25, 2025, which found strong future population and job growth, rising housing demand, and a persistent shortage of units affordable to the lowest-income households. The assessment projects "very strong population growth into the future" and concludes the county will need new homeownership and senior housing units while also addressing rental shortages for younger, lower-paid workers. Key recommendations include establishing a land bank, expanding shelter capacity, and building institutional capacity for affordable housing programs. City Council budget workshops have since centered on addressing affordable housing amid ongoing annexation of hundreds of acres for new residential projects.

ℹ️About TMMK

Toyota Motor Manufacturing Kentucky (TMMK) in Georgetown is Toyota's largest production facility globally, employing approximately 10,000 team members. Since opening in 1988, over 15 million vehicles have rolled off its assembly lines — including the Camry (America's best-selling sedan) and RAV4. TMMK was Toyota's first U.S. plant to manufacture hybrid electric vehicles (2006). Total Kentucky investment now exceeds $11 billion. The facility produces approximately 550,000 vehicles and 700,000 engines annually, supported by more than 400 suppliers nationwide.

🔍Why Did Rates Go Up? The Full Story
1
Engineering flaw halted WWTP1 construction — added ~$20M+
Vendor error · 2021
About six months into construction of Wastewater Treatment Plant 1 (WWTP1), GMWSS engineers discovered the contractor had not specified sufficient structural concrete and reinforcing steel in the plant walls — walls designed to contain large volumes of wastewater. Construction halted in 2021 while the design was corrected and rebuilt. The error significantly increased the total project cost. The final WWTP1 price tag reached $64.2 million, with the plant coming online in 2025. It doubles Georgetown's daily wastewater treatment capacity from 4.5M to 9M gallons per day — necessary to support the city's continued growth. Sources: Lex18 Nov. 2022 ↗ · FOX 56 June 2024 ↗ · Mayor Prather public statement
2
2019 interest rate calculation error — added ~$30M in unexpected debt cost
Consultant math error · 2019
A financial consulting firm made an error in modeling the interest payments on GMWSS's construction bonds in the 2019 rate study. The error caused GMWSS to under-collect revenue for years — the rates set in 2019 were too low to cover the actual interest costs. By the time the error was discovered in 2022, the gap had compounded. Together with the WWTP1 overruns, Mayor Tom Prather (outgoing, Nov. 2022) said the two mistakes totaled approximately $50 million in unexpected costs. GMWSS confirmed it would pursue litigation against both firms to recover losses, though the mayor cautioned any recovered funds would go toward the additional $80M in identified future projects, not customer rebates. Sources: Lex18 Nov. 2022 ↗ · WEKU Dec. 2022 ↗ · Mayor Prather on record
3
15+ years without a rate increase — deferred investment caught up
Structural underfunding · 1998–2019
GMWSS last raised rates in 2007, holding the combined water+sewer minimum at $16.36 for 2,000 gallons from 2007 to 2019 — a 12-year freeze. Council members repeatedly resisted increases by comparing rates to Kentucky-American Water (a large private utility serving Lexington). Meanwhile, the Scott County Fiscal Court invested roughly $25 million in water line infrastructure, growing GMWSS's customer base at minimal capital cost to the utility. That subsidy masked the true cost of operating and growing the system. When large capital projects finally became unavoidable — WWTP1 expansion to serve Georgetown's rapid growth — the deferred investment bill came due all at once. Sources: GMWSS 2022 rate sheet ↗ · Georgetown News-Graphic Nov. 25, 2022 ↗
4
Inflation + rapid growth demand more infrastructure now
Structural · Ongoing
Inflation roughly doubled the cost of water treatment chemicals after 2021. Georgetown is one of Kentucky's fastest-growing cities — driven by Toyota's expanding manufacturing campus and residential development along Paynes Depot Road, Frankfort Road, and other corridors. Water consumption is up approximately 5.7% and sewer consumption up 11% since FY22 (City Council June 9, 2025 ↗ — staff-reported figures). The FY26 budget projects $25.36M in revenue — up 18.59% from FY25 — driven by new depreciation on the $64M WWTP1 investment, increased water purchases during planned plant maintenance, and rising sludge disposal costs. The 2022 Rate Study identified $106.4M in capital needs through 2028 (WWTP1 bonds: $70M, other bonds: $16.4M, cash-funded: $20M).

Key oversight gap identified by residents: Because GMWSS is a municipal utility (not a private utility), it is not subject to the Kentucky Public Service Commission's rate-review process. The City Council is the only body with authority to approve or reject rate increases, but no council member sits on the GMWSS Board. Mayoral candidate Dan Holman raised this structure publicly at the December 2025 council meeting, calling for a review of how the utility is governed. ❓Open Questions Residents Are Raising
Has GMWSS released system pressure data?
Not publicly. Holman stated publicly that pressure above 80 PSI may force leaks through marginal fixtures like toilet valves, contributing to high bills (FOX 56 March 2026 ↗). GMWSS has not publicly released system pressure readings by zone as of March 2026.
What happened with the lawsuits against the engineering and consulting firms?
Mayor Prather stated in 2022 that the city would pursue litigation against both firms responsible for the WWTP1 design flaw and the 2019 interest calculation error (WEKU Dec. 2022 ↗). No public update on the status or outcome of those cases has been reported as of March 2026.
Was there a state audit?
Residents called for a state audit in December 2022. The Kentucky Auditor of Public Accounts responded on February 13, 2023 that no specific allegations of fraud, waste, or abuse had been reported and therefore no special examination was warranted. This was reported at the February 28, 2023 City Council meeting by Councilmember Alonzo Allen (WTVQ Feb. 28, 2023 ↗).
Is the October 2025 open-meetings violation being investigated?
GMWSS acknowledged the incident — board members were locked inside the meeting room after adjournment while the public was locked out — as an oversight, and issued a written statement. It stated no actions were taken after adjournment. Source: Georgetown News-Graphic Oct. 14, 2025 ↗ Whether the Kentucky Attorney General Open Meetings division was notified is not publicly known.
What is the full capital plan through 2028?
The 2022 Rate Study identified $106.4M in total capital needs: $70M in WWTP1 bonds, $16.4M in other bonds, and $20M in cash-funded projects, spread across 2024–2028. The FY26 budget includes $1.79M in departmental capital and $2.65M in Toyota-reimbursed Fund 2 capital. The full 5-year capital plan has not been separately published for public review since the 2022 rate study.
